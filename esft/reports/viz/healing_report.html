Healing k-Expansion Misalignment in Qwen3.6-35B-A3B

Force a top-8 router to fire 32 experts and the model degrades smoothly — not catastrophically. This is a measured account of the damage and of how much a light, router-frozen fine-tune heals it.

All numbers are measured. n = 600 per benchmark (164 for HumanEval), paired exact McNemar, same-machine same-condition arms. July 2026.

1Setup: buying capacity at inference

Qwen3.6-35B-A3B is a Mixture-of-Experts model — 256 experts per layer, trained with top-8 routing, so only about 3B of its 35B parameters are active per token. The router was optimised to pick its best 8 experts and to renormalise their gate weights among just those 8.

At inference we can override that choice and keep more experts. Forcing top-k = 32 activates roughly 6.6B parameters per token — a "A6B" regime — in the hope of unlocking latent capacity the base model already contains. The catch: the model was never trained to co-activate those additional experts, and the gate mass gets renormalised across 32 slots instead of 8. The question this report answers with measurements:

When you switch on experts the model never learned to use together, what breaks — and can a light fine-tune heal it without ever touching the router?

2The damage is monotonic

Base model, no training — only the router's top-k is changed.

Sweeping top-k from the native 8 up to 32 produces a smooth, monotonic decline on both a knowledge benchmark (MMLU) and a math benchmark (GSM8K). There is no sweet spot at 2–3× the native k where extra experts help before they hurt — every step down in accuracy is paid the moment more untrained expert combinations are switched on. The noise grows with the mass of those combinations, and that mass is real: the added ranks 9–32 carry 54.0% of the renormalised gate.

3Healing across three generations

Paired, same-condition comparisons — each patch measured against its own base@k8 on the same machine.

Rather than retrain the router, we heal with selective expert fine-tuning (ESFT): train only residual deltas on the FFN slices of the experts the target corpus actually uses, router frozen (method in §5). Three successively broader training corpora each remove more of the misalignment. The chart shows the gap to base@k8 — how far below the native-routing model each patch still sits — and colours each bar by whether that gap is a statistically significant loss or a statistical tie (exact McNemar).

The pattern: each broader corpus removes more of the misalignment. The agentic-only patch (Gen 1) already heals math to a tie but leaves the knowledge gap significant; adding coding, tool-call, math and a small general/knowledge replay (Gen 2) is what finally pulls MMLU into a statistical tie. The knowledge point estimate is still −2.0 pt — the gap is no longer statistically distinguishable from base, but it has not been driven to zero.

4What training buys beyond healing

The coding axis — HumanEval, n = 164, max_new 4096, local.

Healing is not the whole story. On code, the agentic patch reaches 0.902 on HumanEval — above both base@k8 (0.866) and naive k32 (0.841), and it beats naive k32 with p = 0.041. The mechanism is visible in generation length: the agentic patch compresses reasoning to roughly a third the tokens (median 1,064 vs base's 3,086) while scoring higher. But compression is a knob you can over-turn — a coding-only patch crushed the median to 186 tokens and collapsed accuracy to 0.762, the worst of every arm (its GSM8K fell to 0.820, significantly below even naive k32, p = 0.002).

SFT rewrites behavior before knowledge — corpus diversity is a safety rail, not a luxury.

A single-domain corpus taught the model a style (terse code) faster than it taught it to stay correct: 186-token answers that fail. The mixed corpus with replay is what kept behavior and knowledge moving together. Diversity is not a nice-to-have; it is the guard against training a confident, compact, wrong model.

5Method footnote

How the healing patch is built.

ESFT — selective expert fine-tuning. Profile the router on the target corpus, then select the top-p 0.2 experts by token frequency — 833 of 10,240 layer-experts. Train only the residual deltas on those experts' FFN slices — 2.62B trainable parameters — with the router frozen. No expert the corpus doesn't exercise is touched, and routing behaviour is never altered.

Decontamination. Training data passes exact-match, 13-gram, containment, and benchmark-signature gates with 0 residual hits. The knowledge-replay slice is additionally screened by embedding similarity against the full MMLU test set: the threshold is the p99 of MMLU's own internal nearest-neighbour similarity (0.9922); the maximum observed cross-similarity was 0.939, so 0 examples were dropped — the replay is near-domain, not near-duplicate.
